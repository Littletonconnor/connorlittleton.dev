Author: Henry Hazlitt

Economics In One Lesson by Henry Hazlitt explains the single most important lesson in economics:

> Every economic policy has secondary consequences that often do the opposite of the intended effect of the policy.

Many of the policies/ideas presented in this book, like tariffs, public work projects, taxes, technology, and minimum wage rates overlook secondary consequences by only targeting a specific group that benefits from such policies.

## Broken Window Fallacy

As we will see in the following fallacies, the broken window fallacy is the most persistent fallacy in the history of economics.

The broken window fallacy says that destruction leads to recovery and that recovery creates a boost in the economy. Henry reminds us in the book that a boost to one part of the economy has unforeseen effects on other parts of the economy. In the story of the baker and glazier, the people often forget that the glazier’s net gain in business means the loss of business from the tailor.

## Destruction Stimulates New Business

It is said that destruction like war leads to net benefits because of the enormous need for production. But this fallacy is merely our old friend, the broken window fallacy in new clothing. It confuses need with demand. The more war destroys, the more it impoverishes, the greater is the postwar need. For example, most of the “good” economic results which people at the time attributed to World War II were really owing to wartime inflation.

Those who think the destruction of war increases total “demand” forget that demand and supply are merely two sides of the same coin. They are the same thing looked at from different directions. Supply creates demand because at the bottom it is demand. The supply of the thing they make is all that people have, in fact, to offer in exchange for the things they want. In this sense the farmers’ supply of wheat constitutes their demand for automobiles and other goods. All this is inherent in the modern division of labor and in an exchange economy.

The conclusion is that it is never an advantage to have one’s plants destroyed by shells or bombs unless those plants have already become valueless or acquired a negative value by depreciation.

## Taxes Discourage Production

In our modern world, there is never the same percentage of income tax levied on everybody. The great burden of income taxes is imposed on a small percentage of the nations income. The taxes inevitably affect the actions and motives of those from whom they are taken. Taxing incomes at 50-70% makes people question why they should work and kill a country’s production.

## Public Works Mean Taxes

Everything we get, outside of free gifts of nature, must in some way be paid for. Government spending not only does not make up deficiencies, it diverts resources and manpower that would have otherwise supported jobs in private industries. This is because all government expenditures must eventually be paid out of the proceeds of taxation; that inflation itself is merely a form, and a particular vicious form, of taxation.

While some public works projects create necessary structures like bridges, public works projects that are created merely to provide employment are a detriment to the economy. For every public job created a private job has been destroyed somewhere else.

## Government Credit Diverts Production

All credit is debt. Proposals for increased value of credit, therefore, are merely another name for proposals for an increased volume of debt.

There is a decisive difference between the loans supplied by private lenders and the loans supplied by government agency. Each private lender risks his own money, and people risking their own money are typically a lot more cautious with it. Government loans however do not operate the same way. The whole argument for government agencies entering the lending business, is that it will make loans to people who could not get them from private lenders. This is another way of saying that the government lenders will take risks with other people’s money (taxpayers) that private lenders will not take with their own money.

Government guaranteed home mortgages, especially when a negligible down payment or no down payment whatever is required, inevitably mean more bad loans than otherwise. They force the general tax payer to subsidize the bad risks and to defray the losses. They encourage people to “buy” houses they cannot afford.

Government loans will waste far more capital and resources than private loans. Government loans, in short, as compared with private loans, will reduce production, not increase it.

## Technology doesn’t kill jobs

The belief that machines cause unemployment, leads to preposterous conclusions.

Any jobs that would be lost to more efficient equipment are offset by:

- The jobs of the people who make and repair the equipment.
- Savings in labor costs, which the company could use to expand and create new jobs.
- Increased profits due to greater efficiency, which means that company executives and employees have more money to spend on goods and services that support those merchants.
- Lower prices for products due to greater efficiency, which allows customers to either buy more of those products or support other industries by buying their products and services.

What machines do is to bring an increase in production and an increase in the standard of living. They may do this in either of two ways. They do it by making goods cheaper for consumers or they do it by increasing wages because they increase the productivity of the workers.

## Spreading the Work Decreases Productivity and Employment

The idea behind the "fallacy that efficiency kills jobs" is the belief that if a worker becomes more efficient at their job, it will lead to fewer job opportunities for others. To prevent this from happening, labor unions may implement certain practices to spread work among as many people as possible, such as:

1. Subdivision of labor, where specific tasks are only performed by members of a certain union, even if those tasks are minor aspects of another worker's job. This may involve calling in a separate specialist, such as a tile-setter, to handle a small part of a project. While this may create work for two people instead of one, it also increases the cost of production and takes resources away from other industries.
2. Promoting shorter work weeks with overtime pay past 30 hours. The aim of this strategy is to increase an employee's pay through overtime, or to give employment to other workers who take on the hours that have been cut. However, since this doesn't increase overall production, it can either lead to lower pay for employees, unemployment, or higher prices for goods to cover the increased labor costs.

## Tariffs Support Inefficient Industries

Tariffs are taxes that are imposed on imported goods and are intended to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. However, tariffs often have unintended consequences that can harm the economy and lead to higher prices for consumers. When a government imposes tariffs on imported goods, it raises the price of those goods, making them more expensive for consumers. This can lead to a decrease in demand for those goods and a decrease in the overall level of trade. In addition, tariffs can lead to a decrease in the efficiency and competitiveness of domestic industries, as they are protected from foreign competition. This can lead to a decrease in productivity and wages, as domestic industries are not motivated to improve their efficiency in order to compete with cheaper foreign alternatives. Ultimately, tariffs can harm the economy by decreasing overall production and increasing prices, leading to a decrease in the purchasing power of consumers.

## Exports and Imports

Exports and imports are equal in value because when a country exports goods to another country, it receives payment in the form of currency from that country. This payment can then be used to import goods from the same country or it can be exchanged for the currency of the exporting country. The act of exporting and importing serves to balance trade between countries, as the value of the goods being exported and imported is equal.

However, it is important to note that exports and imports do not necessarily have to be equal in terms of the quantity of goods being traded. For example, a country may export a large quantity of goods to another country and only import a small quantity in return, leading to an imbalance in trade. This can occur for a variety of reasons, including differences in production capabilities and differences in demand for goods between countries.

## Parity Pricing Hurts Consumers

The fallacy behind parity pricing is the belief that increasing the prices of goods produced by a specific industry, such as agriculture, will lead to increased prosperity and full employment. This belief is based on the assumption that higher prices for agricultural goods will allow farmers to purchase more industrial goods, which will in turn support full employment in the industrial sector. However, this assumption is flawed because it ignores the fact that raising prices for agricultural goods often requires reducing the supply of those goods in order to increase demand. This reduction in supply can be achieved through government mandates for reduced production or through government payments to farmers to keep some goods off the market.

The problem with this approach is that it leads to a reduction in the overall supply of goods, which means that the nation as a whole becomes poorer. This is because consumers have less money to spend on other goods and industries lose out on the money that they would have received from those sales. In addition, artificially raising prices can lead to higher prices for consumers, which further reduces their purchasing power and the money available for other industries.

This principle is not limited to the agricultural sector, as the government often adjusts prices in other industries as well. Overall, the use of parity pricing and other methods of artificially adjusting prices leads to a reduction in national wealth and can have negative impacts on employment and production in other industries.

## Price Fixing Have Hidden Costs

The practice of setting price controls, also known as price caps, is intended to help consumers by keeping the cost of essential goods, such as food, at a manageable level. However, in reality, this policy often leads to shortages of these goods. This is because when the government sets a low price on essential goods, consumers are more likely to buy more of them, resulting in increased demand. At the same time, the low price of these goods limits the profits of companies producing them, disincentivizing them from ramping up production to meet the increased demand. As a result, the supply of essential goods becomes constrained, leading to shortages. Inflation, which is often seen during times of war or economic crisis, can exacerbate this effect by causing prices to rise significantly. In an effort to counter this, the government may impose price controls to try to keep the cost of essential goods affordable for consumers. However, this policy ultimately leads to a scarcity of these goods and can have negative consequences for both producers and consumers.

## Rent Control Hurts Tenants

Another form of price-fixing is rent control, which is often viewed as a method to support low- and middle-income tenants. However, in the long run, **rent control hurts not only low- and middle-income tenants, but also landlords, communities, and cities.** Among the consequences, rent control:

- Encourages wasteful use of space by disincentivizing tenants from leaving apartments that may be too big for their needs
- Discourages new housing construction—especially affordable housing—because rent control leaves landlords and builders with little to no profits to invest in construction
- Discourages maintenance and remodeling in rent-controlled buildings, because the lower rents can cut into landlords’ profits so much that they can’t afford to make necessary improvements

## Minimum Wage: The evil in Disguise

Just as artificially raising *prices* hurts productivity and employment, raising *wages* has the same effect. Raising the minimum wage forces companies to do one of two things:

1. Companies can raise the prices for their products, in order to pass the burden to consumers. However, **higher prices can cause consumers to either buy less or find cheaper or alternative goods, which cuts into companies’ profits and lowers employment and productivity levels.**
2. Companies can absorb the higher cost of labor without raising prices on their products. In this case, **marginal companies will go out of business, leading to unemployment and less overall production.**

Additionally, raising the minimum wage doesn’t raise the value of individual workers. When companies see their profits fall as a result of a minimum wage raise, the workers who are deemed unworthy of the higher wage will become unemployed. Although those workers’ previous wages may have been low, they were presumably the best available—and when the workers lose those jobs, they will be forced to accept work and wages that they had previously considered unacceptable.

## The Mirage Of Inflation

inflation is the result of the government taking on a cost that it can’t pay or doesn’t want to pay with tax dollars, so it simply prints more dollar bills. Inflation raises one group’s incomes, which leads to increased demand and higher prices for goods, which raises another group’s incomes, and the cycle continues until the effect has rippled through the economy. **Groups whose incomes rise later are forced to manage rising prices before their incomes have caught up.**
 Additionally, since prices rise proportionately to the rise in incomes, collective wealth does not grow.

## Terms

- **Supply and demand** - Supply and demand are two fundamental forces that determine the price of a good or service. Supply refers to the amount of a good or service that is available for sale at any given time. The more of a good that is available, the greater the supply. Demand refers to the amount of a good or service that people are willing and able to buy. The more people want a good, the greater the demand for it. When supply and demand are in balance, the price of a good or service is stable. If there is a greater demand for a good than there is a supply, the price will tend to rise. If there is a greater supply than there is demand, the price will tend to fall.
- **Depreciation** - Depreciation refers to the decline in the value of a physical asset over time.
- **Inflation** - Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, and, subsequently, purchasing power is failing. Central banks attempt to limit inflation, and avoid deflation, in order to keep the economy running smoothly. Inflation is measured as an annual percentage increase. For example, if the inflation rate is 2%, then a basket of goods that cost $100 in the base year would cost $102 in the current year. There are several factors that can cause inflation, including an increase in the money supply, an increase in government spending, and an increase in production costs. Inflation can also be caused by many external factors, such as a decrease in the supply of a particular commodity or an increase in global demand for a particular good. Inflation can have both positive and negative effects on an economy. On the positive side, it can stimulate economic growth and encourage spending. On the negative side, it can lead to uncertainty and undermine the value of money.
- **Purchasing power** - Purchasing power is the amount of goods and services that can be bought with a unit of currency. It is a measure of the value of money in an economy and is influenced by factors such as inflation, exchange rates, and the overall level of prices in the economy. A person’s purchasing power refers to the amount of goods and services that their income or wealth allows them to buy. If the purchasing power of a currency increases, it means that the same amount of money can buy more goods and services than before, while a decrease in the purchasing power means that less can be bought with the same amount of money. A person’s purchasing power can also be affected by their personal financial situation, such as their savings, debt, and spending habits.
